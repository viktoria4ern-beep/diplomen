Why Expensive Supplements Are a Scam (And the 3 That Actually Work)

The supplement industry is a $170 billion minefield of overpriced placebos, clever marketing, and outright fraud. As a former CEO of a luxury wellness brand, I’m exposing the dirty secrets behind those chic, overpriced bottles—and revealing the only three supplements worth buying.

The Great Supplement Swindle

1. "Clinical Dose" Lies
Most premium brands use dosages far below clinically effective levels (e.g., 50mg of magnesium when studies show you need 400mg).

Red flag: "Proprietary blends" that hide ingredient quantities.

2. The Absorption Scam
Cheap fillers like magnesium stearate (used in 90% of supplements) reduce absorption by up to 60%.

Sneaky trick: "Liposomal" or "nanotech" claims are often meaningless without third-party testing.

3. The "Exotic Ingredient" Con
Rare berries, tropical mushrooms, and marine algae usually appear in doses too small to matter (look for "<50mg" in tiny print).

The Only 3 Supplements You Need

1. High-Potency Magnesium Glycinate
        Why: Reverses insomnia, muscle cramps, and anxiety (unlike cheaper oxide forms).
        Dose: 400mg at night.
        Pro tip: Should cost ≤$0.30 per serving.

2. Vitamin D3 + K2 (in MCT oil)
        Why: Fixes the deficiency affecting 80% of adults—critical for immunity and bone density.
        Dose: 5,000 IU D3 + 100mcg K2.
        Key detail: Must be oil-based for absorption.

3. Omega-3s (Triglyceride Form)
        Why: The only form proven to reduce inflammation and brain fog.
        Dose: 1,000mg EPA/DHA daily.
        Test it: Freeze a capsule—pure omega-3 won’t solidify.

How to Spot Worthless Supplements

Avoid anything with "proprietary blend"

Reject products with more than 5 ingredients (they cancel each other out)

Check for "USP Verified" or "NSF Certified" stamps
